# Five Universal Questions + Full Essay Resources
### Questions apply to all three text pairs. All resources below are for *Nineteen Eighty-Four* + *V for Vendetta* only.

---

## The Five Universal Questions

**Q1:** To what extent do both texts suggest that individual identity can be preserved in the face of systems designed to diminish or erase it?

**Q2:** Both texts explore individuals confronting forces beyond their control. To what extent do they argue that human resilience is sufficient to overcome systemic or environmental adversity?

**Q3:** How do both composers use the journeys of their protagonists to challenge audiences to reconsider what freedom truly means?

**Q4:** To what extent do both texts suggest that the relationship between the individual and the collective determines whether genuine change is possible?

**Q5:** Both texts depict characters under extreme pressure. To what extent do they suggest that it is in the most extreme circumstances that human identity is most clearly revealed?

---

## Essay 1

**Question:** To what extent do both texts suggest that individual identity can be preserved in the face of systems designed to diminish or erase it?

When systems of power seek to define what individuals are worth and what they are permitted to know, the most fundamental form of oppression is not imprisonment but the erasure of the self. George Orwell's 1949 dystopian novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* presents identity as ultimately destroyable: the Party's regime of surveillance, doublethink, and torture dismantles Winston Smith's sense of self until nothing original remains. James McTeigue's 2005 film *V for Vendetta* challenges this conclusion, tracing Evey Hammond's journey from fearful compliance to authentic selfhood and arguing that identity can be rebuilt even after the most extreme attempts at erasure. While both texts identify the self as the true battlefield of totalitarian power, their resolutions reflect distinct historical contexts: Orwell's post-war pessimism against McTeigue's belief in the possibility of individual and collective renewal.

---

## Essay 2

**Question:** Both texts explore individuals confronting forces beyond their control. To what extent do they argue that human resilience is sufficient to overcome systemic or environmental adversity?

The capacity for resilience in the face of overwhelming power is one of the most contested questions political literature can ask, and the answer each text gives reflects the historical conditions that produced it. George Orwell's 1949 dystopian novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* presents resilience as ultimately insufficient: Winston Smith's intelligence, desire, and genuine courage are each absorbed and dismantled by a system designed specifically to consume them. James McTeigue's 2005 film *V for Vendetta* argues to a significant extent that resilience, when transformed from personal endurance into symbolic act, can generate consequences no system of control is equipped to contain.

---

## Essay 3

**Question:** How do both composers use the journeys of their protagonists to challenge audiences to reconsider what freedom truly means?

Freedom is rarely what we assume it to be, and the most challenging task these two texts share is forcing their audiences to reconsider the concept from its foundations. George Orwell's 1949 dystopian novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* redefines freedom at its most essential level: the right to know reality, to assert objective truth, and to exist as a continuous self capable of independent thought. James McTeigue's 2005 film *V for Vendetta* similarly interrogates what freedom requires, arguing through the journeys of V and Evey Hammond that it demands not merely the absence of oppression but the active, collective refusal to consent to it.

---

## Essay 4

**Question:** To what extent do both texts suggest that the relationship between the individual and the collective determines whether genuine change is possible?

Political change requires more than individual courage: it requires the conditions in which individual acts can generate collective consequence. George Orwell's 1949 dystopian novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* presents a world in which those conditions have been systematically eliminated, and where Winston Smith's solitary resistance is precisely the kind the Party is designed to absorb and convert.

---

## Essay 5

**Question:** Both texts depict characters under extreme pressure. To what extent do they suggest that it is in the most extreme circumstances that human identity is most clearly revealed?

Extreme pressure strips away everything a person uses to define themselves and leaves only what is fundamental. George Orwell's 1949 dystopian novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* subjects Winston Smith to the most complete psychological and physical pressure its totalitarian regime can produce, and the revelation is devastating: under sufficient extremity, the self can be not merely suppressed but replaced.
